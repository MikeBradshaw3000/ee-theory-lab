I'd answer Layer 1's fork this way:

Layer 2 recommendation: two rules for the first Layer 3 design-menu contract - A + B.
Not one, and not all three.

Why not one

Starting with only B gets to the most interesting quadrant fastest - high `Psi_meanI_state` / low `Psi_persistence_I` - but it loses the baseline needed to know whether the observed divergence is rule-specific or just an artifact of the apparatus/topology. The Cycle 3 structure has been deliberately built around preconditions and controls; a one-rule first wave would be too thin for that design logic.

Why not all three

All three is scientifically attractive, but it widens the first feedback loop. C is tunable and therefore likely to open parameter-space questions before the baseline contrast is understood. That increases the chance of returning to the Cycle 2 pattern: many moving parts before one clean result.

Why A + B

A + B is the best first wave because it gives a minimal discriminating pair:

* A is the both-agree sanity baseline. It should produce low/low or high/high, but not the unresolved divergence. That lets us verify that the substrate/topology/measurement apparatus can produce non-divergent outcomes under a non-discriminating rule.
* B is the deliberate divergence probe. It is the rule family expected to produce organized active-state configurations that move or shift over time: high `Psi_meanI_state`, low `Psi_persistence_I`.

Together, A + B ask the cleanest first design question:
Can the same topology and measurement apparatus produce both a non-divergent baseline and a genuinely divergent co-equal-observable signature under different local action rules?
That is exactly the right first C3-OBS/C3-INT bridge.

Why not A + C first

A + C is defensible, but I would hold C for wave two. C is more useful after A+B because its value is tunability. Once we know how the apparatus behaves on a both-agree rule and a clean divergence rule, C can ask a richer question:
Can a micro contagion rule interpolate between low/low, high/low, and high/high signatures across Lambda-like variation?
That is a better second-wave question than first-wave question.
